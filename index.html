Welcome !

Hello there! I'm Navin Thapa, a PhD student of Geophysics specializing in Seismology. I'm passionate about studying the Earth's seismic activities and understanding the dynamics of earthquakes and other geological phenomena. I have worked on various seismic research projects, analyzing seismic data, and contributing to our understanding of Earth's structure and seismic hazards. Through my work, I aim to contribute to the advancement of seismological knowledge and promote the safety and resilience of communities in earthquake-prone regions. Join me on this exciting journey as we explore the fascinating world of seismology together!

Numerous geoscientists have confirmed, over the years, that the Earth's ability to adapt to new changes is a characteristic of the planet. I'm always curious about the mechanism behind such alterations; Earthquakes are one of them. In the spirit of embracing my life's mission to serve my community and the world, I decided to pursue a doctorate in seismology. I am from Nepal, which is the central portion of the Himalayas (active tectonic orogeny) and the ideal location for shallow focus, large earthquakes and prone to a perpetual seismic vulnerability. This is the only thing that has ever gotten me interested in the field of seismology. I began my journey at the Tribhuvan University's Central Department of Geology and am now based at the University of Memphis's Center for Earthquake Research and Information (CERI). Research Interest

My interest involves studying repeating seismic activity, fault structures, and earthquake source processes. My research is dedicated to enhancing our understanding of pore pressure, seismicity, seismic b-value, and fault dynamics. To achieve this, I employ various approaches, including laboratory experiments, analytical and numerical models, as well as statistical analyses, with the goal of advancing our documentation in these areas.

I combine insights and techniques from different fields including rock mechanics, earthquake seismology, and hydrogeology. By bringing together these diverse disciplines, I aim to address several important inquiries, including:

  • Understanding the mechanisms of stress accumulation and release in the Earth's crust.
  • Exploring how faults and fault systems respond to minor stress disturbances, such as those caused by fluid injection or extraction.
  • Examining the relationship between large-scale earthquakes and smaller-scale processes, and utilizing these processes to gain insights into fault structures and stresses within the Earth's crust.

    Seismic b-value and pore pressure

    "Seismic b-value and pore pressure" is a fascinating topic within the field of geophysics and seismology. It explores the relationship between seismic activity and pore pressure in the Earth's subsurface.

    The b-value is a seismic parameter that characterizes the frequency-magnitude distribution of earthquakes in a given region. It provides valuable insights into the seismicity and stress conditions of an area. By studying the b-value, geophysicists can assess the likelihood of future earthquakes, understand the behavior of fault systems, and even evaluate the effectiveness of earthquake hazard mitigation strategies.

    Pore pressure, on the other hand, refers to the fluid pressure within the pores and fractures of rocks. It plays a crucial role in seismic activities as it affects the mechanical properties of rocks and their ability to store and transmit stress. Changes in pore pressure can induce stress changes, triggering or inhibiting seismic events.

    The study of the relationship between seismic b-value and pore pressure involves analyzing seismic data, conducting geomechanical modeling, and considering various geological factors. It requires a multidisciplinary approach that combines geophysics, geology, and fluid dynamics.

    Repeating Earthquakes

    "Repeating Earthquakes and Velocity-Weakening/Velocity-Strengthening Material Characteristics"

    Repeating earthquakes are a fascinating phenomenon within seismology that involves a sequence of earthquakes occurring along the same fault segment with similar characteristics. These events provide valuable insights into the behavior and mechanics of fault systems.

    One key aspect associated with repeating earthquakes is the relationship between the fault material properties and the occurrence of these events. This relationship is often described in terms of velocity weakening and velocity strengthening behavior.

    Understanding the relationship between repeating earthquakes and the velocity-weakening/velocity-strengthening behavior of fault materials is crucial for seismic hazard assessment and earthquake prediction. It helps scientists and researchers identify regions with a higher potential for seismic activity and determine the likelihood of earthquake recurrence.

    Studying the mechanics of repeating earthquakes and the material properties of fault zones involves a combination of field observations, laboratory experiments, and numerical modeling. These investigations contribute to the development of seismic hazard models, which aid in assessing the potential impact of future earthquakes and implementing appropriate mitigation strategies.

    Seismic Hazard Assessment

    Seismic hazard assessment is a process that aims to evaluate the potential earthquake hazards in a specific region or area. It involves the analysis of various factors, including historical earthquake data, geological information, and seismological studies, to estimate the likelihood and potential impact of future earthquakes. The assessment is essential for understanding the level of seismic risk and guiding measures for mitigating the potential consequences.

    The process of seismic hazard assessment typically involves the following steps:

  • Data Collection
  • Seismicity Analysis
  • Fault Characterization
  • Ground Motion Prediction
  • Hazard Mapping:Combine the results of seismicity analysis, fault characterization, and ground motion prediction to generate hazard maps. These maps depict the spatial distribution of potential earthquake shaking levels, including peak ground acceleration, peak ground velocity, or spectral response parameters. Hazard maps provide a valuable tool for land-use planning, infrastructure design, and emergency management.

    • Acoustic and Seismic Data Analysis

    The process of acoustic waveform analysis involves several steps to extract meaningful information from recorded acoustic signals.

  • Data Acquition: Acoustic Emission recording in laboratory experiment setup continuously throughtout the experiments.
  • Preprocessing: Preprocess the recorded acoustic waveform data to enhance its quality and remove any noise or unwanted signals. This may involve applying filtering techniques to remove high-frequency noise or using signal conditioning methods to amplify or normalize the signals.
  • Event Detection and Phase Picking: Apply acoustic emission detection algorithms to the preprocessed waveform data. These algorithms are designed to identify and extract acoustic emission events from the background noise. They can be based on various criteria, such as signal amplitude, duration, energy content, or pattern recognition techniques.
